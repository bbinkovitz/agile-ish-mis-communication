What's a Babel Fish?

The Babel fish is a mythical creature that you put in your ear in order to understand any language spoken to you.

Development teams need these.

Requirements, constraints, & assumptions that make sense to product owners can seem like Greek to the developers, and vice versa.

How to communicate

  1. Identify your audience.
  2. Describe each task they need to do.
  3. Include context.
  4. Define "done".

Identify your audience

("Personas" in Agile-speak)

A persona describes one specific category of users.

Examples:

  • A prospective student
  • A logged-in content editor
  • A customer
  • A client app that gets content from your feed or API
  • A forum poster
  • A viewer

Now ask the audience to describe Derrick Davenport as a user.

Describe the need

("User stories" in Agile-speak)

A user story encapsulates one single need.

Examples:

  • "I want to find the application deadlines."
  • "I want to schedule posts for publication later."
  • "I want to add the item to my cart."
  • "I want to find articles about salamanders."
  • "I want to reply to a post."
  • "I want to watch a video."

Now ask the audience to describe Derrick's need to slice bananas for pie, as a user story.

Clarify that they are not describing the banana slicer, but rather his need to have sliced bananas.

Include context

("So that..." in Agile-speak)

Give some background info that hints at next steps.

Examples:

  • "...so that I can apply to the school."
  • "...so that I can maintain a steady stream of published content."
  • "...so that I can review what I’m about to buy."
  • "...so that I can display an arbitrary number of results."
  • "...so that I can participate in discussions."
  • "...so that I can be entertained."

Ask the audience to add a "so that..." describing why Derrick needs all those banana slices.

Define "Done"

("Acceptance criteria" in Agile-speak)

Set the goalposts for when this need is fulfilled.

Examples:

  • "Given the homepage of the site, when I navigate the main menu, then I find a link to admissions information."
  • "Given a saved blog post, when I edit it I can set a publishing date in the future, and when that date arrives the post becomes visible on the blog."
  • "Given a product page, when I click "add to cart" then I can see my cart contents and the new item is included."

Ask the audience to propose acceptance criteria for the banana slicing task.

Mistakes

Common
audience identification
mistakes

  • "As a developer…"
  • "As a developer…"
  • "As a project manager…"
  • "As a developer…"
  • "As a project manager…"
  • "As a website…"
  • "As a developer…"
  • "As a project manager…"
  • "As a website…"
  • "As me…"

The persona exists to keep you focused on your users.

These mistakes indicate a lack of awareness of the target audience… or dishonesty about it.

As a US Army chef...

Consequences of
audience identification
mistakes

  1. Confusion about whose need.
  2. Scope becomes squishy.
  3. Unplanned features for unacknowledged users.
  4. Eleventh-hour changes make the product brittle.
  5. Result meets development needs, not user needs.
Plan in advance how you will balance users needs with stakeholder desires.

“As a member of the board,

so that I can show it off”

Common
task description
mistakes

"I want..." Flaw
"...a website that works" Too vague.
"...[specific JavaScript library, Drupal module, etc.]" Implementation details.
"..it to work like the picture" Picture ≠ task.
"...accessibility." Prerequisite ≠ task.
"...a form for login that redirects me to my page that tells me when I last logged in and asks me to place a new order." Multiple behaviors.

Consequences of
need description
mistakes

These mistakes indicate a lack of a vision about what the users will actually do.

  1. Confusion about what the need even is.
  2. Project scope gets squishy.
  3. Developers clash.
  4. Work is duplicated.
  5. Requirements fall through the cracks.
  6. Developers make guesses about your users' needs.
  7. Product doesn’t behave as expected, has missing parts.

Common
context clarification
mistakes

  • "..." [no “so that” at all]
  • "...so that I can [restates the "I want..." almost verbatim]"
  • "...so that I can [states another, separate need that should be its own "I want..."]"


These mistakes indicate a lack of big-picture cohesion.

Consequences of
context clarification
mistakes

These mistakes indicate a lack of a vision about what the users will actually do.

  1. Confusion about how needs are related
  2. Scope gets squishy.
  3. Developers guess at why your users need the things they do.
  4. Product meets the technical requirements but doesn’t make any intuitive sense.

Common
"done" definition
mistakes

Mistake Example
Unstated expectations "..." (Thinking: "Of course it'll work just like our old one", "Of course it'll work with our third-party API.")
Vague expectations "Given a website, when I do [task], then it works"
Subjective goals "Make it look cute", "make it intuitive", "make it functional"
Adding on more needs "When I [task] then I can also [completely new task]."
Overly technical "When I log in an HTTP request goes to IP address 127.0.0.1 which generates response."

Consequences of
"done" definition
mistakes

These mistakes indicate a lack of a vision about how products are expected to work.

  1. Confusion about how needs are fulfilled.
  2. Schedule gets squishy.
  3. Developers must guess at how the work is evaluated.
  4. Product mostly works but might be late or over budget.

When to communicate

(hint: it's before you think you need to.)

  • Before you talk to developers
  • When the project starts
  • When you have a blocker
  • Early and often

Before
you talk to developers

  • Users+needs drive the project.
  • Record likes & dislikes about other solutions.
  • Prepare with user data
    • What browsers need to be supported?
    • What parts of your site are the most-used?
    • What is "intuitive" for your users?

When there's a
blocker

(part of the project is waiting on another part of the project)

  • Other development tasks
  • Missing information
  • Does NOT include just being unavailable.
  • If you're blocking someone
    • Find out their need.
    • Keep them updated.
    • Don't meddle.
      Don't take back tasks that have already been started even if it seems like it would be faster. In the short run, it might be. In the long run, it creates bad habits and communication overhead.

Scheduling

("Sprints" in Agile-speak)

Schedule = priorities + dependencies.

Sorting

  • "Good enough" vs. "Cool extras"
  • Avoid “do everything first” syndrome.
  • Clicking and dragging items in a list enforces this.

Prioritizing

  • Ordinal prioritization
  • Avoid “do everything first” syndrome.
  • Clicking and dragging items in a list enforces this.
  • If it's not there, it's not done.

Prioritizing

  • Who are you prioritizing?
    • End users?
    • Admin users?
    • The chairperson of your board of directors?
  • What are you prioritizing?
    • Intuitiveness and simplicity?
    • Fanciness and slickness?
    • Robustness and ease of maintenance?

Re-prioritizing

  • It's like brushing your teeth.
  • Don’t change priorities on work that's been started/scheduled.
  • Good recipe for many half-completed things and zero usable components.
  • “Project time” != development time.
